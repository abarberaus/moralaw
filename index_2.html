AREAS OF PRACTICE

  • CRIMINAL DEFENSE

    I regularly represent people accused of crimes primarily in West Texas (El Paso, Culberson, Hudspeth Counties) but I have appeared in all parts of Texas. In Federal Cases, I have appeared nationwide. In military cases I represent Soldiers, Sailors, Airmen and Marines worldwide.

    Do Not Volunteer To Speak To Law Enforcement. Most importantly you must know that anything you say to anyone can and will be used against you. So do not answer any questions until you have spoken to a lawyer. By anyone I’m talking about any law enforcement of course but this includes talking to your close friends, family, spouse or even children. Even answers you might think assert your innocence can be twisted by law enforcement to seem like deception or outright lies. So say nothing! And do not trust what they tell you. It is perfectly legal for an officer or detective to lie to you to get you to talk. You are only legally required to give your Name, DOB, and address - nothing more. When using your right to remain silent you must be clear and specific. Say "I refuse to answer any questions without my lawyer." "I want a lawyer.” Asserting your right to remain silent cannot be used against you in any way – it can’t even be mentioned at trial.

    Military Service members – no one can issue you an order to talk or make a statement. You have the right to remain silent and to speak to counsel under Article 31 of the UCMJ. The problem is that the military often does not give you that lawyer until its too late. Military investigators, commanders, NCO’s will put great pressure on you to break – but don’t say anything until you have had some advice. Asserting your right to remain silent cannot be used against you in any way – it can’t even be mentioned at court martial (trial). I also handle all manner of military administrative cases. These are cases such as Administrative Separations (Chapters), Officer Eliminations, Rebuttals to Letters of Reprimand, AR 15-6 investigations, Show Cause Boards and other adverse actions.

    Do not volunteer anything to law enforcement. Do not volunteer or consent to any search of yourself or your property. If police, MPs or anyone else have a good legal basis for searching you or your property then they can get a warrant. If they search without a warrant do not interfere just continue to make clear that you are not consenting to any search of yourself or your person.

  • FAMILY LAW

    The laws governing divorce and the division of the community estate in Texas are complex and imperfect. Nevertheless, these laws and the decisions of a Judge will have a great impact on your and your children’s life long after the divorce or custody battle is over.

    I see many people who try to navigate the maze of the courthouse themselves. I see the person who falls for the billboard promising a cheap quick divorce or “uncontested divorce”. I see other people who are duped into trying to do it with a pre-prepared form from some website. Divorces in Texas are often more complex and complicated than people appreciate or anticipate. Even when people tell me they have agreed on the house, and shared custody of the kids. My next question is what happens when you two both want little Sally’s on her birthday? And what happens when you want to buy your next house or take out a loan and your Ex hasn’t refinanced the house like he promised. People want things to be simple but there are always details to work out and things that do need to be set in stone and in terms that are enforceable in court.

    Family law is a very local practice. It is my belief that 80% of what a divorce lawyer does is understanding what a judge will do in a given set of circumstances. Judges work hard to be consistent in their rulings. Your case may be unique to you. But the basic issues in dispute between you and your spouse are often very similar to those that the court has heard countless times before. Experience before the Courts is critical. I have over a decade of experience in the family courts of El Paso. My philosophy is to arm you with information so you can make the rational and practical decisions about what is best for you. You need to know what the Judge is likely to do so that you know what is worth fighting for and what is best to settle on.

    Military Divorce. If the breakdown of a family is complicated, the breakdown of a military family presents its own unique challenges. It's likely that you are only in Texas because of your spouse's service obligation. You now find yourself far from family support or even just friend to talk to. Because of my prior active duty experience (including deployments to Europe and the Middle East), I understand the unique challenges and pressure facing a military family. I understand the impact divorce will have not only on the division of your property, including any military retirement, but more importantly how this will impact your children.

    What can I do in the meantime to prepare and protect myself? You should begin to gather important documents. Mortgage statements, tax returns, pay stubs, outstanding bills and credit card statements. Often times the hardest things we have to prove in court are things you might think are the simplest. But your word is not often enough – documentation is key. You should put a fraud alert on your credit report with the major credit bureaus. (Experian, Equifax, TransUnion). You don’t want your soon to be ex-spouse opening new lines of credit and debts only to leave you holding the bag. Gather names and contact information for anyone who you think might be able to offer information about your marriage or the children in case things go south. In short, prepare and leave nothing to hope or chance.

  • APPEALS

    What is an appeal?
    In an appeal, you are not getting a do over. Instead, this process is meant to review legal errors or mistakes in the legal process that occurred in your case.

    Think of an appeal like instant replay review in professional football. In football, the coach only has so much time to throw in the flag asking for a review. You can only review certain plays and the booth can only overturn the ruling on the field if there was some clear error. Appeals work in much the same way.

    Finding the right attorney could make all the difference, and it takes more than your average defense attorney to handle an appeal. An appellate court poses a completely different challenge from the challenges raised by a criminal court trial. You need to work with a legal advocate who understands the ins and outs of a successful appeal through personal experience.

    I do appeals in all areas in which I practice. I do all manner of Criminal Appeals – State, Federal, Military Courts Martial. I do Family Law and CPS Appeals.

    Time is of the essence in appeals. You only have a limited amount of time to appeal. If you believe you need an appeal contact my office immediately to schedule a consultation.

    What do I need to see? I will need to see the Court Clerk’s Record – that record contains all of the pleadings or documents filed by the parties in the case and any orders issued by the Court. You can get that from the Clerk of Court. For example, for felony cases in El Paso County go to the first floor of the County Courthouse to the District Clerk’s Office. There you can order a copy of the Clerk’s Record. I will need transcripts of the trial, any hearings, or other proceedings in the case. That can be ordered from the court reporter for the court where the case was heard. There are fees for both the record and transcripts and it may take some time to produce these records. If you do not have these records I can still offer an opinion but my ability to accurately let you know your chances is greatly increased with more information.

FAQs
Frequently Asked Questions
  • What are your rates or retainer?

    Retainers or fees to retain me as your lawyer will depend on the type of case. I do not have a set fee or flat fee for any particular type of case. I tailor each fee to the amount of work I expect to complete. I do not like to charge people (especially service members or vets) for work I do not intend to do.

  • Do you offer reduced fees for active duty service members or veterans?

    I do take into account your service to our country in determining my fees although I do not have a set fee schedule or rate.

  • Do you offer payment plans or alternative methods of payment?

    I do offer payment plans to clients who request them. However, these plans depend on the amount of time we have to resolve your case and the work necessary to complete it. Some cases may take little or no time to resolve. Unfortunately, in a situation like this, there may be little time to complete a payment plan.

  • Do you have weekend hours or after business hour appointments?

    I do not keep regular weekend hours. I try to reserve that time for my wife and children. But if necessary and by request I will make time to see clients on a case-by-case basis. I understand that sometimes there are few other alternatives. I do, however, check my e-mail regularly and will answer questions promptly if possible.

  • Do you only represent service members or veterans?

    Of course not. I represent anyone who needs help and legal counsel. I have many non-military clients. I will take your case if I think I can help you.

  • Do you accept or subscribe to pre-paid legal services or any other kind of legal insurance plans?

    Generally, no. I am not a participant in pre-paid legal service or insurance.

  • Do you take cases outside of Texas?

    I do take on criminal cases outside of Texas in certain circumstances. It depends on the case and the rules of the court that I am trying to practice before. Some courts have very particular rules about lawyers from other states. Federal courts have rules that dictate how a lawyer from another state may practice before them. I am licensed in Texas and have taken cases in several other states. Do not be afraid to call on me simply because you are from another state in a criminal matter. Family law cases are another matter entirely. Family law is a very local practice. You have to know your local regulations and how your judge rules in any given set of circumstances. If you don't then you are not doing your client any good. It is for these reasons that I limit my family law practice to El Paso County.

  • How much will my divorce or custody case cost and how long will all this take?

    How long this will all take and the costs for your divorce depend on how simple or complex your case is.

  • Do you charge a consultation fee?

    There is no fee for consultations on criminal cases and most military matters. I do however charge consultation fees for family cases and appeals. I do offer reduced consult fees for service-members and dependants.

  • If I put together my own documents can you reduce my fees?

    You must understand that whatever I put my name on I am ultimately responsible for. So I do not provide a document review service. I may consult with you but I will require that you either hire me or don’t.
